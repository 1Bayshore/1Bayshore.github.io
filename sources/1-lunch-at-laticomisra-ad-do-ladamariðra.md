*(This is the first installment of a new series called Livusle bestar an Mangátle that will be posted every Sunday. I hope you enjoy it!)*

Zešemkon stood just inside the door to Laticomisra ad do ladamariðra, pressed up against one of the wood panelled walls. Her eyes darted back and forth between the counter some distance across the room and the door. She fidgeted with her grey dress as she waited, running the linen slowly back and forth along her left hand.

A woman wearing a dark red shirt walked over to Zešemkon. "Can I help you with something?" she asked.

"I'm waiting for a friend," Zešemkon said, staring down at the ground.

"If you'd like," the woman said, "I can seat you now and your friends could join you at the table."

"No thanks," Zešemkon said.

"Okay," the woman in red said. "Let me know if there's anything else I can help you with."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. The woman in red hurried off across the café.

After a short bit of time, Juþea walked through the door, wearing an orange shawl and a green skirt. She looked around for a moment, before spotting Zešemkon and walking over to her.

"Hi Zešemkon!" Juþea said. "How are you doing?"

"I'm doing alright," Zešemkon said, looking up from the floor. "How about you?"

"I'm doing well," Juþea said. "I've got one more exam, and then I'm on break."

"That's nice," Zešemkon said. "Which one is it?"

"History of the Ancient Land, unfortunately," Juþea said.

"I thought you liked that one," Zešemkon said.

"I mean, it's an interesting subject," Juþea said. "But since we had to cover over 3000 years, we spent the entire course beginning to discuss something interesting and then immediately moving on. And the exam is entirely short-answer questions, with no chance to elaborate on anything."

"Oh, I'm sorry," Zešemkon said.

"It's okay," Juþea said. "Are you doing anything interesting soon?"

"Not really," Zešemkon said. "I've been having to fill in for Larte at the till."

"I'm so sorry," Juþea said.

"Yeah, it's pretty scary," Zešemkon said. "But he has Espa's disease, so it's not his fault."

"Oh dear," Juþea said. "I hope he gets better soon."

"Me too," Zešemkon said.

The door opened and Minatli walked in. She looked around and spotted Juþea and Zešemkon, then hurried over to them. "Hello!" she said, her voice rising and falling a lot as she spoke. "How have you been?"

"I'm doing well," Juþea said. "I'm almost on break. I've just got one more exam to finish."

"That's exciting," Minatli said. "Speaking of being almost finished, I've sent in Dokagrolis Pilgrimage for review."

"Congratulations!" Juþea said.

"Nice," Zešemkon said.

"Thanks," Minatli said.

"Do you know what you'll do next?" Zešemkon asked.

"Probably The Four Sailors," Minatli said. "Cosli bought the Adcovtle translation rights last year."

"That's nice," Zešemkon said. "I want to read it when you're done."

"Thanks," Minatli said. "It might be a while though - it's almost 200 pages."

"That's okay," Zešemkon said. "I can read something else in the meantime."

The woman in red walked back over to Zešemkon, Juþea, and Minatli. "Are you ready to be seated now?" she asked.

"We are," Juþea said.

"Great," the woman in red said. "Let me show you to a table."

Zešemkon, Juþea, and Minatli followed the woman the short distance to a table. Zešemkon took the chair on the left side against the wall. Juþea took the chair next to Zešemkon, and Minatli sat across from Juþea. The woman in red then handed each of them a menu. "Here's our lunch menu," she said. "I'll be back in a moment to take your order." She then hurried off to help another table.

Juþea didn't even look down at the menu. "You know what I'm getting," she said.

"The JTC stew?" Minatli said and Zešemkon mumbled at the exact same time.

"Exactly," Juþea said.

Minatli looked over the menu. "I wonder what the soup of the day is," she said. She looked around the room for the waitress, who she spotted behind the counter adding dirty dishes to a stack besides the sink. She then looked back down at the menu. "If it's just a bisque, I think I'll get the beef and pepper stew."

Zešemkon quietly peered over the stack section of the menu. "Do they have rice bread here?" she asked Juþea.

"They should," Juþea said. "Honestly, since I moved here, I've rarely seen a restaurant without rice bread."

"Is it different in Vlojtum?" Zešemkon asked.

"Very different," Juþea said. "It's hard to get rice at all. I knew someone who would take day trips to Glisng to buy rice in bulk."

"Wow," Zešemkon said. "I didn't realize it was that hard to find up there."

"It really is," Juþea said. "The person I knew who did those day trips to Glisng said that rice existed in groceries in Covtle roughly everywhere east of the Jaþmanda and south of the Ecvri, and the rest of the country it's impossible to find."

"Interesting," Zešemkon said. She flipped over the menu and began to read through the teas.

The waitress walked back over to the table. "Are you ready to order, or do you need more time to decide?" she asked.

"I think we're ready," Juþea said. She looked at the other two. Zešemkon nodded.

"I have a question, and then I'm ready," Minatli said.

"For sure," the waitress said.

"What is the soup of the day?" Minatli asked.

"It's a creamy potato and tomato bisque," the waitress said.

"Ah," Minatli said. "I'll get the beef and pepper stew then."

"The beef and pepper stew," the waitress said. "Okay." She turned to Juþea. "What would you like?" she asked.

"I'd like the JTC stew," Juþea said.

"The JTC," the waitress said. "Okay." She turned to Zešemkon. "What would you like?"

"Do you have rice bread?" Zešemkon asked, while staring at the menu.

"Yes," the waitress said. "Although it's 75 titoþa extra."

"Okay," Zešemkon said. "I'll get the tomato and cheese stack on rice bread, please."

"The tomato and cheese stack on rice bread," the waitress said. She looked about the table. "Is that everything?"

"Um..." Zešemkon said. The waitress looked back at her. "Did you want something else?"

"A small double Dyaša tea," Zešemkon said. "Please."

"A small double Dyaša tea," the waitress repeated. "You're certain you want something that strong?"

"Yes," Zešemkon said, glancing up at the waitress before looking back down. "I like tea."

"My apologies," the waitress said. "One small double Dyaša tea. Does anyone else want a drink?"

"Tap water, please," Juþea said.

"Tap water as well, please," Minatli said.

"Excellent," the waitress said. She gathered up all of the menus. "I'll be back with your food shortly." She hurried off towards the kitchen area.

"I wish people didn't make assumptions like that," Minatli said to Zešemkon. "I'm sorry about that."

"It's okay," Zešemkon said. "Thanks though."

"Have either of you read any good books lately?" Juþea asked.

"Dokagrolis Pilgrimage," Minatli said. "Obviously, I guess."

Two people stood up from another table. One of them, a woman in an orange and blue striped dress, walked over to Zešemkon, Juþea and Minatli. "Hello, Juþea," she said. "What a coincidence to see you here."

"Hi, Kósènà," Juþea said to the woman in the striped dress. "I'm just getting lunch with my friends Zešemkon and Minatli." She turned to Zešemkon and Minatli. "This is my classmate Kósènà. She's in the same programme as me."

"Hello Kósènà," Minatli said. "I'm Minatli."

"I'm Zešemkon," Zešemkon said, staring at the table.

"Nice to meet you," Kósènà said. She looked at Minatli. "I feel like I've seen you before. Were you at the cultural festival at the end of last year?"

"I was!" Minatli said. "Funnily enough, my work, Cosli translations, wanted someone to be there and hand out business cards and jade codes, so I was paid to go to a festival I wanted to go to anyway."

"That's awesome," Kósènà said. "On the topic of business cards, I'm working for a self-support organization called Wàmèrì e Adivusle ad Mangátle, or Weaam." She took two cards out from her handbag. "We just opened an office on Lagan ad laseñata ini, if you ever need cultural support."

Minatli glared at Kósènà. "I appreciate the offer," she said slowly and firmly, "but I am Adcovtle."

"Oh, I'm sorry," Kósènà said. She looked over at Zešemkon, who was pressing herself up against the wood-panneled wall. Zešemkon glanced over at her, then went back to staring at the table.

"I'm- I'm sorry," Zešemkon said. "I'm not really Šelva Enkan. I was reborn- I mean born- in Mangátle."

"It's nice to run into you," Juþea said to Kósènà. "I'm sure I'll see you after the exam."

"Nice to run into you too," Kósènà said. "Good luck with the exam!"

"Good luck to you too," Juþea said. "Bye!"

"Bye," Kósènà said. She hurried off and out of the café.

As soon as she had left the building, Minatli let out a long sigh of frustration.

"I hate my stupid accent," she said. "I say a single word, and people are like 'aha! She must be Wàmèrì!' I'm so close to telling them I'm from Ongrèstilsa just to confuse them."

"I'm really sorry," Juþea said. "Like you said earlier, people shouldn't make assumptions like that."

"Yeah, but it's neverending," Minatli said. "Especially when I work in translation. 'Oh, you translate Wàmèrì literature into Adcovtle?' Not if I have any say in the matter! And then they're shocked that I speak Meēu Gēo and Šelva Enkan. 'I've never met a Wàmèrì person who speaks Šelva Enkan!' I don't think they'd be asking the same question to an Adcovtle person who speaks five languages with an Adcovtle accent!"

"I mean," Zešemkon said, "I hope this is helpful, but she did assume I wasn't Adcovtle either."

"Maybe she just assumes everyone who seems the slightest bit foreign isn't Adcovtle," Minatli said. "Ironic but fitting, for someone who works in a immigrant self-support organization."

"Yeah," Zešemkon said.

Minatli smiled. "I'm glad you two understand me," she said.

"Thanks," Juþea and Zešemkon said.

The waitress returned, carrying a plate and two bowls. "The JTC stew," she said, setting down a steaming bowl of yellow-orange stew in front of Juþea. "The beef and pepper stew," she said, setting down a bowl full of chunks of beef and peppers in a clear broth. "And the tomato and cheese stack on rice bread," she said, setting the plate down in front of Zešemkon.

"Thanks," Juþea, Zešemkon and Minatli said.

"I'll be back in a moment with your drinks," the waitress said. She hurried back towards the kitchen.

"Thank you two for being here," Juþea said. "I know it's silly, but I'm always glad to see you, whenever we get a chance to hang out."

"You're welcome," Minatli said, smiling.

"Thanks Juþea," Zešemkon said. "I'm glad to have you two as friends."

"So am I," Minatli said.

The waitress returned, holding a mug with a steaming deep red tea and two glasses of water. "Small double Dyaša tea," she said, setting it down in front of Zešemkon, "and two tap waters." She set one water in front of Juþea and one in front of Minatli.

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. She took a sip of the tea.

"You're welcome," the waitress said. "Is everything correct?"

"Yes," said Juþea, followed by Minatli, followed by Zešemkon.

"Wonderful," the waitress said. "Let me know if you need anything else." She then hurried off to another table.

And without further ado, Juþea, Minatli, and Zešemkon began to eat.
