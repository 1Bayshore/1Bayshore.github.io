4: The last drop

26 March 2026
Previous Next

The living room was fairly modest in size, with the long distance that it stretched back from the front door matched with a skinny width. A long olive green sofa filled the majority of the area to the right, while the remainder of the space made a sort of cooridoor that led from the front door to the back of the room. There, a doorway on the left opened into the kitchen, while a steep staircase to the right of the doorway led upstairs. A small window beside the door provided the only natural light in the room, with the walls to either side barren of windows, and instead adorned with low-quality prints of the Emerald Palace, the Old Tower at the University of Mangátle, the Great Bell of Wimþac, and the like, each labelled below in non-matching handwriting that was more decorative than legible.

An older man wearing a white shirt, black pants and a blue jacket sat at the end of the sofa closest to the door. A younger woman wearing a white shirt and black pants sat next to him. A middle-aged man wearing a blue shirt and black pants sat at the other end of the sofa.

"I actually lost points for that," the younger woman said. "My instructor said that while the layout was extremely clear to understand, that I had used 82 zeps more gold than the current minimum example, and anything over 8 zeps per tilinjatþod more was an automatic deduction of 10 percent."

"That's a shame," the older man said. "You've demonstrated such potential, to lose 10 percent for only being 2 zeps over seems a bit harsh."

"I don't think that's too harsh," the younger woman said. "It's a clearly defined rule from the beginning of the course. Besides, excess gold usage is the number one reason why layouts aren't successful in production. It quickly makes them unaffordable."

"You make me proud," the older man said. "To have a granddaughter who is so dedicated that she will continue to strive for better, even when she is already the best in her class, that brings serious pride to our family."

"I do my best to make up for our family's embarrasment," the younger woman said.

Zešemkon slowly descended the stairs, wearing her usual grey linen dress and carrying a backpack. The older man looked at her. "Va tapalavi tetablac (transl. You mentioned bears)," he said. "We were just talking about you." Zešemkon continued down the stairs without even looking up.

"On the topic of layout affordability," the younger woman said, "are you concerned about the Łok'a embargo?"

"It's a provocation, that's for certain," the older man said. "I doubt it will have serious impact on gem costs." He looked at Zešemkon again, who had reached the bottom of the stairs and was now walking along the cooridoor to the front door. "Come sit down," he said. "I haven't had the opportunity to talk to you this entire visit."

"I'm meeting a friend," Zešemkon said, without looking up, as she continued to walk towards the front door.

The grandfather stood up and positioned himself to block the door. "Your sister and I are only visiting you and your parents for three days," he said. "I will not stand any longer for you ignoring us to spend time with people who you can see any day of the year."

"Father-in-law," the middle-aged man said, "there's nothing we can do here. She's being rude, but she's not a child anymore."

"Just because she's an adult doesn't mean she's immune to all discipline," the grandfather said, remaining in front of the door. "I know that our country has become afraid of demanding respectful behaviour, but living in Wàmèrà really shows you how it's done." Zešemkon reached her grandfather and stopped in front of him, looking down at her feet. Her grandfather, towering over her, looked down at her as he continued to speak. "In Wàmèrà, when children turn 18, they have to spend three years serving their country in the armed forces. And they do not mess around in there. If you dare to refuse an order, they- Why do you have blood running down your leg?"

"I'm dead," Zešemkon said. She touched the drop of blood running down her left leg with her left index finger, getting most of the blood onto her finger.

"Stop lying to me!" her grandfather said. Zešemkon looked at the blood on her finger for a moment, before beginning to flick it off. "You're- Stop that!" He hurredly moved to his right and away from Zešemkon. "You're going to get blood on me!" He looked down at his white shirt and spotted a tiny red speck. "You disgusting girl!" he exclaimed. He tried to pull the area with the tiny red speck outwards from his body without touching the speck. "That nearly touched me!" He hurried around to behind Zešemkon and stormed towards the stairs. "Once I'm changed out of these clothes," he said, looking back at Zešemkon, "I'm teaching you how to..." He trailed off as Zešemkon opened the front door, ran through, and slammed it behind her.

Zešemkon stepped down the three stairs from the stone terraced house and turned left on the small street. She walked along it, glancing at the near-identical gardens of orange roundflowers that grew in the tiny spaces between the terraced houses and the street. She giggled slightly. A man walked the other way down the street, stumbling and weaving his way along the street. He said "hello" in a long, drawn-out manner. Zešemkon ignored him and walked on.

Zešemkon reached an intersection with another street, and turned left. She walked a short distance down it until she reached a street with a tram track on it. A crowd of people waited beside the track near the intersection. She waited near the back of the crowd, flicking her fingers back and forth with her thumb and looking down the track to her right.

Several people in the crowd in front of her were chatting with each other excitedly. "Do you think today will be better than Three Weeks at Sea?" a tall man wearing an orange lalabnol ave that hadn't been sinched closed and a flower-patterned skirt said to short man wearing an identical outfit.

"I doubt we'll see something better than Three Weeks at Sea this year," the short man replied. "I've never seen a better lalinjatþod oðla (transl. hollow emerald) on stage."

"I agree we won't see a better satire," the tall man said. "However, I'm intrigued by the stage adaption of The Inconvenient Family Farm."

"I've never read the book," the short man said. "Although it reminds me of..."

Meanwhile, a woman wearing a patterned red and purple dress and carrying a large clay flowerpot full of dirt but no visible plants was talking to young boy carrying a much smaller clay flowerpot without any plants in it.

"You're going to get to choose which seed you want to plant," the woman said. "You'll have to think about which one you want."

"I want food!" the young boy said.

"We'll get food after we buy some seeds," the woman said.

"I want food now!" the young boy said.

"It's important to be patient," the woman said. "First you need to decide what seed you want to plant."

"All of them!" the young boy said.

The woman sighed. "If you plant too many seeds, they won't be able to grow," she said. "You need pick only one seed..."

The tram came around the curve, making a slight scraping sound as it slowed to a stop. The gold-coloured paint was just beginning to chip off, showing a bit of green paint underneath. The door opened and the crowd began to board the tram. Zešemkon got out a 10 titoþa coin and paid the conductor as she stepped up into the tram.

The interior of the tram was packed. Every seat was taken, and people were standing from the very back almost all the way to the driver's compartment at the front. Zešemkon grabbed a hold of a pole just behind the front door of the tram, and held on as the doors folded closed and the tram lurched and started forward down the street. She looked up at the advertisments above the windows, and began to read them along the right side of the tram.

The first advert had a picture of a bowl of ice cream with becisua spread on top, along with the slogan "Orasleda: Not just for warming. Are va ðadelañate epsante. (transl. Ask for flavour.)"

Their adverts really are on every tram, Zešemkon thought.

She scanned the next adverts. There was one for discounted rice bread, that somehow cost more than the non-discounted rice bread at the grocery where she worked. A hard-to-read advert with yellow text on a beige background said "Home electricity: Fole Enkan 100%; Quərfi 94%; Wàmèrà 81%; Covtle 12% . Campaign to move Covtle beyond gemstone power." The furthest back one she could read on the right side of the tram showed a picture of a sink with the tap running and said "When water won't work, try fire."

I wonder what they're trying to sell, Zešemkon thought.

The tram slowed to a stop, and the doors were opened again. A few more people tried to squeeze into the already packed tram. After they had paid, they crowded in the small space between Zešemkon and the conductor. Zešemkon pressed herself up against the pole, squished between the tall man in the flower-patterned skirt and a woman trying to calm a crying baby by gently rocking them, rubbing the back of her shirt against Zešemkon's face several times in the process.

When the tram reached the stop for Laðlini ad tezudba e teþodgo cleðza (transl. The Art and Natural History Museum), Zešemkon squeezed by the woman with the baby, down the stairs, and out of the tram. She walked on the crowded pavement next to Lagan ad lacalcí (transl. Wall Street), following the tram tracks along the street with its unique blend of wall-to-wall buildings to her left and the walls of back gardens across the street. She weaved her way through the crowd of people turning onto or off of Lagan ad latlamo Caþonlo (transl. Lord Caþonlo Street) and over to Lacaþblom veci (transl. South Square).

Zešemkon looked around the crowded square. Market stalls were set up throughout the square, with huge cultivated flowers, paintings, illusory expanding paintings, woven baskets, flower bulbs, and wooden recorders for sale. Endless crowds of people weaved about the square, crossing between the stalls and across the tram tracks that ran through the sqaure.

I'm never going to find Juþea in this crowd, Zešemkon thought. Which part of the square do I start looking in? I guess we normally meet on the south side. She started walking, weaving her way through the crowd as she walked south across the square towards the mountains. She took a moment to look up at the mountains longingly, before hearing a tram ring its bell as it slowly moved through the square. She ran to the other side of the tracks, and looked around through the crowd. Suddenly, she spotted Juþea, standing next to where Lagan ad lahocatofvej ini (transl. New Rememberence Street) left the sqaure. Zešemkon walked quickly towards her, waving at her to get her attention. As she got close, Juþea waved back. Zešemkon walked up to Juþea and stopped.

"I was worried I wouldn't see you in this crowd," Juþea said.

"Me too," Zešemkon said.

"Where do you want to go?" Juþea asked.

"Somewhere less crowded," Zešemkon said.

"Should we start walking up Lagan ad lahocatofvej ini?" Juþea asked.

"Yes please," Zešemkon said.

They began walking up the winding street between the large stone houses, set back from the street and surrounded by gardens with short stone walls. The noise of the crowd began to fade behind them. As they rounded the first curve, Juþea said, "How have you been?"

"Bad," Zešemkon said. "My grandparents and my sister are visiting."

"Oh no," Juþea said. "I'm so sorry."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. "My grandfather even tried to keep me from leaving to meet you."

"He is such a bullet-headed piece of arsenic," Juþea said. "I'm so sorry that you have to deal with him."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. "I managed to get him to flee the room again."

"How so?" Juþea asked. "If you don't mind me asking."

"Same way as last time," Zešemkon said.

Juþea broke into laughter. "I can't believe you did that again," she said.

Zešemkon smiled a bit. "It was even funnier than last time," she said. "He started telling us how Covtle needed to force people into the military like Wàmèrà, and then still fled the room because of the drop of blood."

Juþea started laughing again. "What a adnorbuj hypocrate," she said. "By the way, do you need a tampon?"

"Yes, please, thanks," Zešemkon said.

Juþea took off her backpack and dug through it for a moment. "Here's one," she said, pulling one out. "Although," she said, looking around at the houses and gardens, "I'm not sure where you could get some privacy."

"The woods up the hill are good," Zešemkon said.

"If you're okay with that," Juþea said.

"I am," Zešemkon said.

Juþea swung her backpack onto her back again, and they began walking up the hill again.

"How have you been?" Zešemkon asked.

"I've been good," Juþea said. "I did well on my last exam."

"Yay!" Zešemkon said. "I'm glad it went well."

"Thanks," Juþea said. "I finished out my semester with an average of 9.8."

"That's great!" Zešemkon said. "I've never gotten above an 8.5 on anything."

"I didn't mean to brag," Juþea said. "Honestly, you're brilliant, especially when it comes to nature."

"I'm not upset," Zešemkon said. "And thanks."

"You're welcome," Juþea said. She looked up ahead at the large bushes at the end of the street, where the large houses and gardens stopped and the woods began. "Speaking of nature," she said. "here's the end of the street."

Zešemkon smiled and shoved her way through the bushes and into the woods. Juþea began to shove her way through the bushes, before pulling back and instead climbing the short wall around the garden to the left, walking along it, and then leaping down onto the ground next to Zešemkon.

"Sorry," Juþea said, whispering her ws and shortening her vowels. "Pushing through the bush was unwise due to my living body."

Zešemkon laughed. "Of course, Queen Juþea," she said. "I forgot how fragile the living are."

"I'm not fragile," Juþea said, continuing the accent. "I'm only Adglisng." They both broke into a long bout of laughter.

They walked a short distance through the woods. Zešemkon looked back towards the houses. The trunks of the tress, the bushes in the understory, and the emerging leaves made it look as if there was nothing human-made for a long distance. Juþea glanced back as well.

"Do you think this is private enough?" Juþea asked Zešemkon, back in her normal accent.

"Yes," Zešemkon said.

"Great," Juþea said. "Do you want me to look away?"

"Not unless you want to," Zešemkon said.

"Okay," Juþea said, not turning away.

Zešemkon started. "I saw a bear on my walk last atada," she said.

"That's exciting!" Juþea said. "How far up the mountain was it?"

"Near my favourite tree," Zešemkon said. "It went downhill after it saw me."

"That's good," Juþea said. "I haven't seen one that far down in quite a while."

"Hopefully we'll see more," Zešemkon said.

"I sure hope so," Juþea said.

Zešemkon finished, and began walking up the hill again. "I was reading a book that said that Northern Bears are doing even worse," she said.

"That's definitely true," Juþea said. "My grandfather said there used to be Northern Bears around Norbuj when he was a child. Now they've left the Wiþvada valley entirely."

"That's even worse than the book said," Zešemkon said.

"It must be a bit out of date," Juþea said.

"Probably," Zešemkon said. "I also read a novel about someone who finds a static levitation gem and uses it to build a floating train."

"How?" Juþea said.

"They just place them on the ground every 10 calej instead of using a track," Zešemkon said. "It's fiction."

"Do you want to hear why that wouldn't work?" Juþea said. "I know it's fiction, but still."

"Yes please," Zešemkon said, smiling.

"Okay," Juþea said. "So first of all, the closest thing to a static levitation gem is magnetite, and it only levitates other magnetite, and sometimes it does the opposite and sticks to the other one. And even if we somehow found a static levitation gem, if you only placed them every 10 calej, you'd be thrown up and down like crazy. That is, if you were somehow able to make a sapphire..." She trailed off as she saw Zešemkon stop and look upwards at a small bird with a blue head and top and a grey underbelly.

"It's a riverhopper," Zešemkon said quietly. "I haven't seen one since last spring."

"Exciting," Juþea said quietly.

The riverhopper sang a song, which sounded roughly like "Ihiu aiu ihihí (transl. ee-ee-oo aa-ee-uu ee-ee-ee!)." Another bird somewhere else in the forest repeated the song. The riverhopper in the tree above Zešemkon sang the song again, and the other bird repeated it again. The riverhopper then flew off.

"Thanks for waiting," Zešemkon said. "That was super exciting."

"No problem," Juþea said. "That was exciting to see. Do you want me to finish?"

"Yes please," Zešemkon said.

"Okay," Juþea said, "so the third problem is that even if you made the train levetate, to push it forward you'd have to make a sapphire push on air, which is extremely inefficient, and would take hundreds of huge emeralds to even get across town, let alone anywhere useful. And the track would have to be perfectly straight, because if it turned even slighly there wouldn't be any wheel flanges to keep you from sliding off the side. Is that overthinking the fictional premise enough?"

"Remember Mount Tliðune after dark ?" Zešemkon said.

"Oh right," Juþea said, chuckling. "I still haven't seen any of those eyeless owls."

"Or human-carrying vultures," Zešemkon said.

"Not to mention a treeline in the Cientla mountains," Juþea said.

Zešemkon laughed. "I liked the mowed grass at the top," she said.

"By the way," Juþea said, "how far are we planning to go today?"

"Is the top okay?" Zešemkon said.

"Absolutely," Juþea said. "I enjoy spending time with you, and since I'm on break I have no obligations today."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. "I'm having fun."

"Also," Juþea said. "I want to make sure you know that if you ever need somewhere to spend the night away from your family, you can come over to my place."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. "I'll be okay tonight."

"That's good," Juþea said. "Just know it's an option, whenever you need it."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. "I'll let you know."

They continued to walk up the mountain. Birdsong filled the air again, from birds that were out of sight. Zešemkon looked excitedly at a white squirrel darting through the forest, stopping to watch it. Juþea stopped as well, smiling.
