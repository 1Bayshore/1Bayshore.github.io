7: When a grey dress is not a dress

1 April 2026
Previous Next

﻿The large 4-8-4 steam locomotive pulled onto the large red-painted iron arch bridge over the wide river, pulling a long train of dark green carriages. On the side of the river the train came from, a few battered ruins lined the tracks, with the land beyond stretching out in an endless series of sand and shrubs. On the side the train was crossing towards, a massive city of stone, bricks, and especially glass rose high into the sky. A few farm fields grew alongside the river on the side with the city, before the land became drier once more.

Inside one of the carriages, Zešemkon, Juþea, Þana, and Atleva sat on two brown seats facing each other. Zešemkon and Juþea were facing backwards, while Þana and Atleva were facing forwards. A folding bunk above them was stowed away. They were talking.

"It's wonderful that you're so knowledgeable about butterflies," Atleva said. "Have you ever considered raising them as a business?"

"No," Zešemkon said, watching out the window. "I've never raised animals before. I just meet them outside."

"That's fair," Atleva said. "Keeping butterflies is something of a trend right now. Our friends Caþa and Jura have started raising them in their greenhouse."

"Nice," Zešemkon said.

"Is there something that you want to do?" Þana asked.

"I don't know," Zešemkon said. "I just work at a grocery."

"Interesting," Þana said. "What do you do there?"

"Mostly stock shelves," Zešemkon said.

"It might sound simple," Juþea said, "But she's really good at it. I've never seen someone with a better memory of where things are in the shop."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said.

"Have you ever thought about-" began Atleva.

An annoucement in Senta Grèstilsa came over the speaker system. Zešemkon and Juþea couldn't make anything of it other than that "Dyaša" was mentioned twice, but when it finished, Þana said, "We're arriving! Also, they said that we'll go through customs after we exit the train."

"I hope they don't need my eye colour again," Zešemkon said, smiling slightly.

"I'm sure they won't," Juþea said. "That's such a Grèstilsan question to ask."

"It still makes no sense," Zešemkon said. "Most of my eyes are white, not red."

"Agreed," Juþea said. "And it's a terrible way to tell people passing through Grèsent apart, since it's Grèstilsans who have colourful irises. So the very people who need to apply for the transit visa are by definition the least likely people to answer anything but brown."

"At least mine aren't brown," Zešemkon said.

"Well, but that's because you're... special," Juþea said.

"So are you," Zešemkon said.

Juþea looked at her for a second. "Thanks," she said.

"You're welcome," Zešemkon said.

The interior of the carriage darkened as the train reached the end of the bridge and passed onto an elevated track with the tall buildings towering above it. The squealing of the brakes got louder, and the train moved slower and slower, until after a moment the train passed over several points and pulled into a platform in a large station. Juþea peered eagerly out at a boxy red locomotive with a large pantograph pressed up against an overhead wire. The train came to a full stop.

Everyone in the carriage began to gather their belongings and form a queue. Zešemkon, Juþea, Atleva and Þana did the same. "I wish they wouldn't gender separate the sleeping carriages," Þana said as the sound of the doors being opened passed through the carriage, and the queue began to move. "Now we have to wait for Clisuðe and Plosuna on the platform. And I don't know what we'd do if Iste was on the trip."

"It's a very Grèstilsan thing to do," Juþea said.

"I can't argue with that," Þana said.

The four of them made their way along the carriage walkway to the end and out the door onto the platform. A man in a red cap motioned for people to move along the platform towards a staircase at one end, but several groups waited on the platform. As the four of them came to a stop in the middle of the platform, Juþea took a few steps closer to the red locomotive, before exclaiming, "The track is dual-guaged at this station!"

"What?" Zešemkon asked.

"Look at the track," Juþea said excitedly. Zešemkon did so. "There's three rails rather than two, because trains in Grèsent use Wiþi guage like in Covtle, but trains in Fole Enkan use the wider Enkan guage, and-"

"Hi everyone!" Plosuna said. Atleva, Þana, Juþea and Zešemkon looked over towards him. He was standing next to Clisuðe, and holding his camera to his eye. He clicked the shutter, then lowered the camera. "I thought I'd get a photo of us in the station."

"Maybe you should give us an opportunity to look at you first?" Þana said.

"Good idea," Plosuna said. "I'll take another one." He started to wind the camera. "Oh dear, I'm out of film." He stood for a second. "Do you remember where I packed the film canisters? I seem to have forgotten."

"Probably the large trunk," Þana said.

"Thanks," Plosuna said.

The platform was beginning to empty as most of the people still waiting made their way towards the staircase. "We should get going," Þana said.

"Okay," Plosuna said. He put the lens cap back on the camera, and the six of them started moving down the platform as directed. They ascended a black metal staircase onto a walkway and followed their way along until they reached a large queue. They waited, moving forward slowly but regularly. Clisuðe asked Plosuna a question about the camera, and soon they were back into rapid-fire conversation. Zešemkon began to fidget with her dress as they waited.

After a little bit, the group in front of them was called forward, and Zešemkon, Juþea, and her family could see the concrete room ahead of them. Four people in red caps and formal uniforms stood behind desks some distance apart. Each of them was checking the documents and luggage of the people standing in front of the desks. There was a red line on the floor just ahead of Zešemkon, Juþea and her family, and the stowed luggage was being brought in by another person in a red cap from a side door. Zešemkon saw Juþea's family's trunk being brought in.

The group in front of the man furthest to the right was directed to pass into the hallway beyond, and the man at that desk then beaconded for Zešemkon, Juþea, and her family to come forward. Atleva indicated to the trunk as they passed it, and it was brought up to them. The man behind the desk said something in Fole Enkan, then immediately added "Hello, what brings you to Fole Enkan?" in Adcovtle.

"Hello," Atleva said. "We're visiting Dyaša as tourists this week, and leaving on lograda."

"Could I see your passports, please?" the man asked.

Everyone began to hand him their passports. Meanwhile, Atleva said, "One of our group joined last minute and accidentally left her passport behind. Could we make an attestation for her?"

"It depends," the man said, setting the passports down on his desk. "Which one of you is it?"

Atleva pointed to Zešemkon, who looked down at the floor.

"Okay," the man said. "Woman in grey, can you come forward?"

Zešemkon walked forward, looking at the passports on the desk.

"What is your name?" the man asked.

"Zešemkon," Zešemkon said quietly.

"What is your city of residence?" the man asked.

"Mangátle," Zešemkon said.

"Okay," the man said. "Do you have any identity documents with you at all - birth certificates, healthcare certificates, death certificates, official notices of residence..."

"No," Zešemkon said.

"Okay," the man said. "Who is willing to make attestation for Zešemkon?"

Juþea stepped forward. "I will," she said.

"Okay," the man said. He opened the passports, looking through them until he found Juþea's. He looked carefully at Juþea, then at the photo, before looking down at the information and saying "Oh, okay. I see." He looked through the other passports, holding them up and checking the photos against the rest of the group. He then handed back the passports and pulled out a blank piece of paper and a pen from the desk drawer. He wrote a couple words on the top in Enkan script that Zešemkon and Juþea couldn't read, then handed the pen to Juþea and said, "You and Zešemkon, please sign your names on this paper."

Juþea signed her name, then handed the pen to Zešemkon, who did the same. "Is that it?" Juþea asked.

The man put the paper and pen back in the drawer. "Do you have any alcohol in your luggage?" he asked.

"No," Atleva said.

"Then have a nice visit to this lovely city," he said, and indicated for them to pass onwards. They started to, until Clisuðe and Plosuna saw the trunk was not being moved and picked it up themselves.

"Why can't they have a porter help with this part?" Clisuðe said as they carried it along the hallway and around a corner, repeatedly banging it into themselves as they walked.

"No idea," Þana said.

They reached a lift and a staircase going down. Þana pressed the lift call button, and the door opened to a large lift with solid metal walls. They dragged their luggage into it, and Þana looked at the labels of the buttons for a moment, which were entirely in Enkan script, before selecting the lower of the two. The door closed and the lift descended for a short bit before it stopped and opened onto a busy concourse full of people. Concrete dominated the architecture of the concourse, with shops throughout having concrete walls and accentuated by glass. Clisuðe and Plosuna began to drag the trunk out of the lift, before a woman with a rolling luggage rack carrying a few suitcases approached them, and asked a question in Fole Enkan. Þana gave a stumbling response, and the woman picked up the trunk seemingly effortlessly and placed it on the rack.

"She's taking our luggage to the Katyu Hotel," Þana explained, as she handed the woman her suitcase. Atleva and Clisuðe also gave their suitcases to the woman, while Plosuna said, "Wait! I need to get another canister of film from the trunk." and threw open the trunk, beginning to dig through its contents.

Meanwhile, Atleva turned to Zešemkon. "Do you have any idea why the border officer asked if you had a death certificate?" she said. "It seems rather odd."

Zešemkon looked down at Juþea's feet. "I don't know," she said. "I mean, he is-"

"Clisuðe!" Plosuna said, pulling his head out from the jumbled mess of the trunk's contents. "Did you take the film canisters out of the trunk?"

"No," Clisuðe said. "I don't remember you putting them in the trunk though."

"That's preposterous!" Plosuna said. "I put them in the trunk, and the two of us travelled with it to mums' house, and then we packed the rest of the trunk-"

"You left that trunk at our place," Atleva said. "This is our trunk, which you added to once the butler had brought yours in."

Plosuna looked at the trunk contemplatively for a moment, before shoving the contents back in, closing and fastening the lid, and stepping away. "I urgently need to visit a camera shop," he said.

The woman with the luggage rack said something to Þana in Fole Enkan. "Oh dear," Þana said. She looked at the rest of the group. "Does anyone have any Tsiledyas?"

"No," Atleva said. Everyone else remained silent.

The woman with the luggage rack said something else. "Oh good," Þana said. She pulled out a 1 zelum coin from her handbag and gave it to the woman with the cart. They had one more brief exchange and then the woman set off.

"Where are we going now?" Plosuna asked.

"Is anyone else hungry?" Atleva asked.

"Yes," everyone responded one by one.

"Then let's find a restaurant," Atleva said. "Ideally somewhere nicer than the station."

Zešemkon looked around the busy concourse. To the left of the elevator they had come down in, there were several glass doors. Through the glass she saw a wide street, with a few cars moving slowly along it, and many people walking alongside it. Across the street there were a few buildings with signs, all in Fole Enkan and unreadable to Zešemkon.

"I see a restaurant over this way," Þana said, beginning to walk towards the glass doors. The rest of the group began to follow her. They passed through the doors and out onto the large pavement beside the street, which was actually quite skinny for cars. "It looks like they serve traditional Enkan food." She walked over to the street and began to cross, upon which a loud blaring horn came from a very bright red car to her left. As soon as she stepped across the white line in the centre, the car sped past, even as the rest of the group approached the street.

"Why are they in such a hurry?" Juþea said. "It's the centre of the city! I'm supprised they're even allowed to drive here!"

Nevertheless, the group watched to their left as they crossed, only for another blaring horn to come from their right. A black car was sitting very close to where they were crossing. Juþea sighed as they reached the pavement on the other side.

Þana walked up to the door of the building in front of them. "This is it!" she said, opening the door and holding it as everyone else got there. They then stepped inside.

Zešemkon immediately felt that something was off about this place. She looked around, to see several rows of shelving, several tills at the front, and an eyestraining amount of bright overhead lights. Everyone stood there for a moment. A woman in black and red walked over to the group and said something in Fole Enkan. Þana responded, and after a few more words exchanged the woman left. Þana looked at the rest of the group. "Sorry," she said. "I misunderstood the sign. This is a grocery, not a restaurant."

"That's okay," Atleva said.

"The woman did say there was a good restaurant down the street to the right," Þana said.

"Let's go there then," Atleva said.

They exited the grocery and turned right. As they began to walk along the street, the tall glass and concrete buildings, elevated walkways and railways, and crowds people walking quickly along the street filled the view. Zešemkon looked around for greenery, but none was in sight.

A silver train rumbled along an elevated railway some distance ahead of them. Juþea looked at it excitedly. "That's a Dyaša metro!" she said.

"Nice," Zešemkon said. "Are we going to ride one today?"

"I sure hope so!" Juþea said. "We'll probably need to, to get to our hotel."

"Here's the restaurant," Þana said. She walked over to a dark blue door that was propped open. Everyone else followed her into the building.

In stark contrast to the grocery, this building was carpeted in red, and filled with tables of various sizes with black tablecloths, with people eating food at several of them. A woman stood next to the entrance. She said something in Fole Enkan. Þana responded in Fole Enkan, then the woman said something again, then Þana repeated herself. The woman then nodded and led everyone over to a large round table with eight chairs. A few of the other people looked over from their food at them for a while, before turning back to their meals. Everyone began to take a seat, Zešemkon next to Juþea, Atleva and Þana adjacent themselves, and Plosuna and Clisuðe next to each other, as was becoming a pattern it seemed. The woman then left.

"They have a waiter on staff who speaks Adcovtle," Þana said. "So you won't have to rely on my poor Fole Enkan."

"You're better than you say you are, sweetie," Atleva said.

"Thanks," Þana said.

Zešemkon looked at Juþea. "I need the washroom," she said. "I'll be back when I'm done."

"Okay," Juþea said. "Do you think you can find it without knowing Fole Enkan?"

"I think so," Zešemkon said. She pushed back her chair and stood up.

"The word for washroom starts with a character that's a loop that's been struck through with a line," Þana said.

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. She walked over towards the back of the restaurant, and though a doorway into another room.

Shortly after Zešemkon left, a man walked in from a different doorway and approached Juþea's family's table. He was carrying several menus. "Hello," he said in Adcovtle. "My name is Hyaktše and I am your waiter today." He distributed the menus to the people at the table. "Is there a sixth person?" he asked, as he found himself holding one more menu.

"She's in the washroom," Atleva said.

"She's sitting here," Juþea said, indicating to the seat to her left. The waiter set the last menu down in front of Zešemkon's chair.

"Is there something you want to drink?" the waiter asked.

Everyone glanced down at the menus. "I'm sorry," Clisuðe said, "but I can't read Fole Enkan. Do you have an Adcovtle menu?"

"I apologise, we do not," the waiter said. "Is there a type of drink you want?"

"Do you have any tea?" Þana asked.

"Yes, we have tea," the waiter said. "Our most popular is Dyaša tea, but we also have Imperial tea, wine fusion tea, afternoon blend tea, and many others."

"I'd like afternoon blend tea," Þana said.

"I'd like that as well," Atleva said.

"Do you have cider?" Plosuna asked.

"I apologise, we do not," the waiter said. "We have several wines."

"What wines do you have?" Plosuna asked.

"We have many," the waiter said. "We have Enkan wines such as Lagon Special, Lagon White, Upland Šelva, and Hyalrantye, but also world wines such as long-aged Wàmèrì wines and Bleøtle wine. I make recommendations easier if you have a specific meal in mind."

"What food would you recommend?" Plosuna said.

"Our pork is excellent," the waiter said. "I recommend our slow-roasted pork with a rich Dyalno-inspired seasoning and a choice of side between noodles, soup, or bread."

"That sounds delicious," Plosuna said. "I'll order that, please."

"Excellent," the waiter said. "I recommend Upland Šelva wine with that meal."

"I'll order that as well, please," Plosuna said.

"Excellent," the waiter said. He turned towards Clisuðe. "Is there a type of drink you want?" he asked.

"Do you have flavoured waters?" Clisuðe asked.

"We have two," the waiter said. "We have pear flavour and šenua fruit flavour."

As the waiter spoke to Clisuðe, Zešemkon walked back from the washroom. She passed through the doorway into the room where Juþea's family was sitting, flicking her thumbs against her fingers as she walked back towards her seat. As she got close, the waiter glanced at her, looked back at Clisuðe, then looked back at Zešemkon again. He slowly took several steps away from her, and as she sat down at the table he stepped further back from the table.

"I'll get šenua fruit flavour," Clisuðe said.

"Šenua... fruit... flavour," the waiter said, his eyes continuing to stare at Zešemkon. Zešemkon looked down at the table and began fidgeting rapidly with her dress.

"Are you okay?" Þana asked the waiter.

"There's someone in- in a grey d- d- d-" the waiter said.

Þana looked at Zešemkon, then back at the waiter. "I didn't see anything at the door banning people from wearing a grey dress," she said.

"It's not a d- dress," the waiter said. "It's a d- d- death shroud." His eyes remained fixed on Zešemkon.

Þana looked at Zešemkon, confused. "Do you have any idea what he's talking about?" she asked.

"I'm dead," Zešemkon mumbled.

"You are?" Þana said. "I had no idea." The waiter took another step backwards.

"Can I help explain?" Juþea asked Zešemkon.

"Yes please," Zešemkon said. Juþea offered her hand to Zešemkon under the table, and Zešemkon clasped it.

"So," Juþea said, looking at everyone but especially Þana, "Zešemkon's a kind of undead person called a Šahyalte, who maintains some lifelike functioning despite having died." At the word Šahyalte, several people in the restaurant began to stare at her, and one knocked over a glass onto the floor, which shattered. "She's a Layastše specifically, meaning she was already dead at birth." The waiter took another step backwards. "As you've seen," Juþea said, looking around at her other family members, "being undead doesn't prevent her from being the sweetest person I know. It does have an impact on her diet, although she still eats food."

"Okay," Þana said. She looked over at the waiter, who by this point was some distance across the room. "Maybe you could take her drink order now?"

"But- but- she's dead," the waiter said.

"As my daughter explained," Þana said, the irritation in her voice growing, "she still eats food. We were just placing our drink orders, maybe you could finish?"

"You don't understand," the waiter said. "If I bring her food, she- she- she'll take my soul."

"What?" Þana said. "As my daughter just explained, she's a very sweet person. How would she possibly take your soul?"

"I can't take souls," Zešemkon said, a bit louder this time.

Þana looked back at the waiter. "She just explained that she can't take souls," she said. "Now, could you please bring us our drinks?"

"But she's from the- the next world," the waiter said. "She's- she's-"

Atleva glared at the waiter. "We're not here to argue about theology," she said. "We're here because we're hungry, and are trying to order food. If you can't take our orders, could you please send someone who can?"

"But- but- she'll take their soul," the waiter said, stepping backwards again.

"So," Þana said, "are you saying you're not willing to serve our friend like the rest of us?"

"You're- you're- I'm- she's dangerous," the waiter said.

"You don't know her!" Juþea exploded. "I've known her for three years now, and she's the best friend I've ever had! She's kind, curious, funny, and not dangerous in the slightest! And now you're telling me that she's dangerous without even speaking to her?"

The waiter stood still for a moment, before turning and running away through a doorway at high speed.

Þana stood up from her chair. "This is outrageous," she said. "If they can't treat their customers with respect, then we shouldn't be eating here." She turned to Zešemkon. "I'm so sorry that he treated you like that," she said. "You deserve so much better."

"Thanks," Zešemkon said. Tears began to stream down from her eyes.

"I'm sorry," Juþea said to Zešemkon, as she began to cry herself. "You really are the sweetest person I know, and anyone who tells you otherwise is a piece of arsenic."

Everyone else at the table stood up, and began to move towards the exit. Zešemkon and Juþea were the first out the door of the restaurant. Still crying as they stood on the pavement, Juþea asked, "Do you want a hug?"

"Yes please," Zešemkon said, and they wrapped their arms around each other. Zešemkon felt Juþea's tears falling on her shoulder, and Juþea felt Zešemkon's tears. The rest of Juþea's family came out of the restaurant and waited quietly until Zešemkon and Juþea let go. As their tears began to dry, Þana spoke again.

"Do you want to find another restaurant that's more respectful?" she asked.

"Can we buy food at a grocery?" Zešemkon asked.

"I'm not opposed," Þana said, "but I'm not sure how we'd make it. We don't have access to a kitchen. "

"If you're hoping to avoid waitstaff," Juþea said, "maybe we could find a place that sells food to go."

"That could be nice!" Zešemkon said. "Maybe we could eat at a park then."

"We can try to find one," Þana said.

"Thanks," Zešemkon said.

"Do you know of any parks near here?" Juþea asked.

"I don't know of any," Atleva said. "But we should walk some direction. We certainly won't find one standing still."

"Maybe we should go back to the station and get a map?" Juþea suggested.

"I'm sure we'll be fine," Atleva said. She turned and began walking alongside the road away from the station. Juþea and Zešemkon looked at each other for a moment, then everyone began to follow Atleva.

As the group continued down the street, they passed a number of other businesses on both sides of the road. The buildings were a bit plain, mostly made of concrete, bricks, and glass. Zešemkon and Juþea couldn't tell what they were for the most part, although one was clearly a bakery, given the loaf of bread on its sign and the smell of fresh bread that wafted out from it. Þana kept an eye out for takeaway restaurants, but didn't stop the group as they reached the first cross street. Cars and lorries sped both ways along the cross street, leaving no opportunity to cross. A man coming from the right ran past them, turning back the way they had come. After waiting for a moment without a gap in the traffic, Atleva said, "Let's turn right, I guess."

The rest of the group followed her along the side of the street full of cars. After a moment Þana said, "Everyone, I just spotted a sign about a currency exchange in this shop. We should stop and exchange some of our money."

"Excellent," Atleva said. She smiled at Þana. "Thanks."

Zešemkon and Juþea followed Þana, Atleva, Clisuðe, and Plosuna into the shop. A bell hung from the door jingled as they opened the door.

The interior of the shop was packed with items. The small space, surrounded by white walls, contained a counter to the right, with a woman in a red shirt who was sitting behind it, looking extremely tired, three units of shelves, not counting the shelf behind the counter, and shelves all along the walls, except for what looked like a refridgerator interrupting it. She shelves were stacked with all manner of items - dried noddles, handkerchiefs, paper flowers, and film canisters. Unsurprisingly, Plosuna practically ran towards the last item.

Þana approached the counter. The woman looked up at her, and said something slowly in Fole Enkan. After a brief conversation, Þana set a large pile of zelum coins on the counter. The woman slowly counted them up, entered something into the large and complex cash register, and exchanged them for Tsiledya banknotes. Þana said something to her and turned back to the rest of the group.

"We're good to get going," she said.

"Just a moment!" Plosuna said. He carried a massive pile of film canisters over to the counter and clumsily set them down. "I'd like to buy these, please," he told the woman.

The woman looked at the cansisters, and then back at Plosuna, staring at him for quite some time. Þana walked over. "How many is that?" she asked.

"Eleven," Plosuna said.

Þana got out some Tsiledyas and gave them to the woman behind the counter. "You can pay me back in zelums," she said to Plosuna.

"Thanks," Plosuna responded.

The woman behind the counter eventually stopped staring and rang up the transaction.

"Is he going to use 11 canisters of film?" Zešemkon asked Juþea quietly.

"Probably," Juþea said. "He's done this before."

Zešemkon looked a bit dumbfounded as she, Juþea, and Juþea's family left the shop.
